# Lexington, Massachusetts

## The Birthplace of American Liberty

On April 19, 1775, ordinary men became extraordinary. They stood on a village green, facing impossible odds, and changed the course of history.

---

## Why Lexington Matters

Before dawn on April 19, 1775, approximately 77 militiamen—farmers, craftsmen, shopkeepers—assembled on Lexington Green to face nearly 700 British regulars. Captain John Parker, 45 years old and dying of tuberculosis, made the decision to stand.

What happened next remains disputed: someone fired "the shot heard round the world." When the smoke cleared, eight Americans lay dead and ten wounded. The British continued to Concord, but Lexington's sacrifice had already transformed a political crisis into a war for independence.

Why does Lexington matter today? Because it reminds us that liberty has always required ordinary people willing to stand in its defense. The men on Lexington Green weren't professional soldiers. They were neighbors. They stood not because victory was assured—it wasn't—but because their community asked it of them.

## Essential Sites

### Lexington Green (Battle Green)

The actual site where the first shots were fired. The Minuteman Statue by Henry Hudson Kitson (1900) stands where Captain Parker's militia formed. The Revolutionary Monument (1799) marks the grave of the fallen.

- **Open:** Always accessible
- **Cost:** Free
- **Time needed:** 30-45 minutes
- **Accessibility:** Fully accessible pathways

### Hancock-Clarke House

Where Paul Revere arrived shortly after midnight to warn Samuel Adams and John Hancock. Reverend Jonas Clarke's parsonage was the hiding place of these radical leaders—and their escape route when the alarm came.

- **Open:** Seasonal (April-October, check website)
- **Cost:** Admission fee
- **Time needed:** 45-60 minutes
- **Accessibility:** Limited (historic building)

### Munroe Tavern

British headquarters during the retreat from Concord. Here, exhausted soldiers regrouped before the final march to Boston, harassed by colonial militia.

- **Open:** Seasonal
- **Cost:** Admission fee
- **Time needed:** 30-45 minutes
- **Accessibility:** Limited

### Buckman Tavern

Where Lexington's militiamen waited through the cold hours before dawn. When the alarm bell rang, they emerged from this tavern to take their places on the Green.

- **Open:** Seasonal
- **Cost:** Admission fee
- **Time needed:** 45-60 minutes
- **Accessibility:** Limited

## Stories

### Well-Known: Captain Parker's Choice

John Parker faced an impossible decision. His men were outnumbered nearly ten to one. Retreat would be prudent; standing would be principled. According to tradition, he told them: "Stand your ground. Don't fire unless fired upon, but if they mean to have a war, let it begin here."

Parker survived the battle but not the year—tuberculosis took him that September. He never saw the independence his men died for that morning.

**Verification status:** Verified (person and events); Oral Tradition (exact words)

### Lesser-Known: Prince Estabrook's Wound

Among those standing on the Green was Prince Estabrook, an enslaved man. He was wounded in the fighting—one of the first Black men to bleed in the American Revolution. His presence raises profound questions: Why did an enslaved person fight for liberty denied to him? What did he hope for? The historical record offers no answers.

Estabrook served throughout the war and was eventually freed. He died around 1830, having outlived most of the Revolution's famous names.

**Verification status:** Verified (basic facts); Questions remain about motivation and experience

### Lesser-Known: Jonathan Harrington's Doorstep

Jonathan Harrington lived in a house facing the Green. When the British fired, he was shot through the body. Rather than die where he fell, he crawled—across the Green, toward home. His wife watched as he dragged himself to their doorstep, where he died at her feet.

**Verification status:** Anecdotal (details from later accounts; basic facts verified)

## Plan Your Visit

### Getting There

- **From Boston:** 11 miles northwest via Route 2
- **Public Transit:** Bus from Alewife Station (Red Line terminus)
- **Parking:** Available near the Green and at visitor centers

### Best Time to Visit

- **Patriots' Day** (third Monday of April): Annual reenactment draws thousands
- **Spring-Fall:** Pleasant walking weather
- **Weekdays:** Fewer crowds at historic sites

### Suggested Itinerary

**Morning (2-3 hours):**
1. Start at Lexington Visitor Center for orientation
2. Walk the Green, visit the memorials
3. Tour Buckman Tavern (where militia waited)
4. Tour Hancock-Clarke House (where Revere delivered warning)

**Afternoon extension:**
5. Continue to Concord (5 miles) via Battle Road Trail
6. Visit North Bridge (where British were turned back)
7. Return via Munroe Tavern
