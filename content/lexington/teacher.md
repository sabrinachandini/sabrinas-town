# Lexington, MA — Teacher Module

## The American Revolution in Lexington

**Grade Range:** 5-12 (adaptable)
**Estimated Duration:** 2-3 class periods
**Standards:** CCSS.ELA-LITERACY.RH, C3 Framework (see detailed alignment below)

---

## Overview

Lexington offers more than a battle site—it's a lens for examining how ordinary people make extraordinary choices. This module guides students from basic historical knowledge to critical analysis of evidence, perspective, and the construction of historical memory.

---

## Essential Questions

1. **Why did events in Lexington matter to the Revolution?**
   - Beyond "first shots fired"—what did Lexington symbolize?

2. **How do different sources tell different stories about the same events?**
   - Comparing contemporary accounts, later memories, and artistic depictions

3. **Whose voices are missing from the historical record?**
   - Women, enslaved people, dissenters—what can silence tell us?

4. **What would you have done?**
   - Moving from observation to moral imagination

---

## Learning Objectives

Students will:
- Explain the significance of Lexington in the American Revolution
- Analyze primary sources from the Revolutionary period
- Compare perspectives of different historical actors
- Connect local history to broader Revolutionary themes
- Evaluate the reliability of different types of historical evidence

---

## Lesson Plan

### Warm-Up (5-10 minutes)

**Activity:** Project an image of Lexington Green today. Ask:
- "What do you know about this place?"
- "What questions do you have about its history?"
- "What might have happened here?"

Record responses for later comparison with what students learn.

### Direct Instruction (15-20 minutes)

**Content:**
1. The situation in April 1775—tensions, British military presence, colonial preparation
2. Paul Revere's warning and the muster on the Green
3. The confrontation: what we know and what remains uncertain
4. The eight who died and their community
5. Connection to Concord and the wider war

**Teaching note:** Resist the temptation to present a seamless narrative. Highlight uncertainty where it exists. "Someone fired first—we don't know who."

### Guided Practice (20-25 minutes)

**Activity 1: Primary Source Analysis (Groups)**

Distribute excerpts from:
- Paul Revere's deposition (Patriot perspective)
- British officer accounts (from later memoirs)
- Town records documenting preparations

Using the analysis worksheet, groups examine:
- Who created this source and why?
- What perspective does it represent?
- What does it tell us? What doesn't it tell us?

**Activity 2: Timeline Construction**

Students create a timeline of April 18-19, 1775, noting:
- What we know happened (verified events)
- What tradition tells us (unverified details)
- What remains unknown (gaps and questions)

### Independent Practice (15-20 minutes)

**Assignment:** Graphic organizer comparing perspectives

Students choose two figures connected to Lexington (options: John Parker, Paul Revere, Prince Estabrook, Dorothy Quincy, a British soldier) and complete:
- What did this person likely know?
- What did they fear?
- What did they hope for?
- How might they have experienced the morning of April 19?

### Closure (5-10 minutes)

**Exit ticket:**
- "What surprised you most about this history?"
- "What questions do you still have?"
- "If you stood on Lexington Green in 1775, what choice would you face?"

## Primary Sources

### Source 1: Paul Revere's Deposition (excerpt)

*[Massachusetts Historical Society, circa 1775]*

> "I set off, it was then about 11 o'Clock, the Moon shone bright. I had got almost over Charlestown Common, towards Cambridge, when I saw two Officers on Horse-back, standing under the Shade of a Tree..."

**Analysis prompts:**
- Why did Revere write this account?
- What details does he include? What might he omit?
- How does this compare to Longfellow's famous poem?

**Credibility tier:** Tier 1 (Primary source, participant account)

### Source 2: Captain Parker's Words (traditional)

*[First recorded decades after the event]*

> "Stand your ground. Don't fire unless fired upon, but if they mean to have a war, let it begin here."

**Analysis prompts:**
- These words appear in no contemporary source. Why might that be?
- Why do you think these words became famous?
- Does it matter if they're exactly accurate?

**Credibility tier:** Oral Tradition (widely repeated, late documentation)

### Source 3: British Account (Ensign De Berniere)

*[Later memoir]*

> "On our coming near to them they fired one or two shots, upon which our men without any orders, rushed in upon them, fired and put them to flight..."

**Analysis prompts:**
- How does this differ from American accounts?
- What might explain the differences?
- Which account should we believe? Why?

**Credibility tier:** Tier 2 (Participant account, written later)

## Comparative Assignment

### Connecting Lexington to the Revolutionary Network

**Instructions:** Research how Lexington's Revolutionary story connects to ONE other town from the list below. Create a presentation or essay showing:

1. What happened in each town
2. How the towns are connected (shared people, events, routes, themes)
3. How understanding both towns together changes your understanding of each

**Comparison towns:**
- Concord, MA (battle continuation)
- Boston, MA (where the expedition began)
- Philadelphia, PA (where news electrified the Congress)

**Rubric:**

| Criteria | Excellent (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) |
|----------|--------------|----------------|----------------|
| Historical accuracy | All facts verified, sources cited | Most facts accurate, sources noted | Some errors, incomplete sourcing |
| Analysis depth | Insightful connections, multiple perspectives | Clear connections, some analysis | Surface-level only |
| Use of evidence | Multiple primary sources integrated | Some primary sources used | Limited evidence |

## Discussion Questions

### For younger students (5-8):
- Why do you think people remember Lexington?
- What does it mean to "stand your ground"?
- If you had to choose between safety and standing up for something, what would you think about?

### For older students (9-12):
- The Revolution claimed to be about liberty. Prince Estabrook was enslaved. How do we understand this contradiction?
- Captain Parker's famous words may be apocryphal. Does historical myth serve a purpose? When is it problematic?
- Who gets remembered in history? Who gets forgotten? Why?

### For advanced students:
- Compare Lexington's place in American memory to how other nations remember their founding conflicts. What patterns do you see?
- How has the meaning of Lexington changed over time? (Consider: 1799 monument, 1900 Minuteman statue, current interpretation)
